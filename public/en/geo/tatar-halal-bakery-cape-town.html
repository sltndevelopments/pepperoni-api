Frequently Asked Questions — Cape Town Buyers

Is your halal certification accepted in the South African market?

Our certificate is issued by DUM RT — the Muslim Spiritual Board of Tatarstan, an internationally recognised Islamic authority. The certification framework mirrors the criteria applied by SANHA (South African National Halaal Authority). All products are 100% pork-free and alcohol-free, with full ingredient traceability available on request.

How long does sea freight from Kazan to Cape Town take?

Transit runs 30–40 days via the port of Durban, then onward road freight to Cape Town. Products ship deep-frozen at −18 °C with 180-day shelf life, so the transit window presents no quality risk. We ship EXW Kazan and can recommend experienced freight forwarders with Russia-South Africa routes.

What is the minimum order for a first export shipment?

MOQ starts at one pallet per SKU. For restaurants or distributors wanting to trial the full Tatar pastry range, we offer mixed-SKU pallets combining echpochmak, peremyach and gubadiya. Pricing is quoted in ZAR or USD; payment terms are discussed individually.

Can packaging be adapted for the South African market?

Yes. We produce Private Label (SBM) packaging in English and Arabic with your brand identity. Standard export cartons already carry full English ingredient declarations and allergen information. Custom portion sizes — for example, individual street-food servings of peremyach — can be developed for fast-casual concepts in Cape Town.

Which Cape Town restaurant formats are best suited to Tatar pastries?

Echpochmak and peremyach are natural fits for halal fast-casual, food halls, and market concepts — Cape Town's V&A Waterfront and Oranjezicht City Farm Market are exactly the kind of venues where novelty halal street food drives footfall. Gubadiya works as a signature dessert or sharing plate in full-service restaurants targeting the city's affluent Muslim dining segment.

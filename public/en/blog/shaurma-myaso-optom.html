Shawarma Meat Wholesale: Filling Types, Slicing, Supply

Shawarma meat wholesale for food service establishments is the foundation of a successful street food business. Choosing the right supplier, meat type, and slicing technology directly affects the product taste, cost, and customer loyalty. This article covers key aspects of wholesale shawarma meat purchasing: from raw material selection to supply logistics in Kazan and Tatarstan.

Types of Shawarma Meat: Halal Options

For establishments working with halal products, several meat types are available. Beef remains the classic choice thanks to its rich flavor and firm texture. It is ideal for traditional recipes and pairs well with spicy sauces and seasonings.

Chicken is the most popular option in the mass segment. It has a neutral taste, cooks quickly, and costs less than beef. For shawarma, thigh or breast fillet is used, pre-marinated in special blends.

Turkey is gaining popularity as a dietary alternative. It contains less fat but requires more careful selection of spices for a rich flavor. Lamb is used less frequently due to its specific aroma, but is popular in establishments with traditional Eastern cuisine.

Combined Filling Options

Many manufacturers offer blended options: beef with chicken or turkey with beef. This balances flavor and reduces product cost without quality loss. Proportions are typically 70/30 or 60/40 in favor of the more budget-friendly component.

Important: When purchasing halal meat, require certificates from recognized Islamic organizations. Documents must confirm compliance with all Sharia requirements at all production stages.

Shawarma Meat Slicing Technology

Proper slicing is critical for final product quality. Meat is cut into thin slices 2-4 mm thick strictly across the grain. This ensures quick cooking and tender texture. For industrial production, slicers with adjustable thickness are used. It is important that pieces are uniform for even cooking on the vertical spit.

For optimal results, the meat should be slightly frozen before slicing — this allows achieving the thinnest possible slices without tearing. After slicing, the meat goes through the marinating stage, which may last from 2 to 24 hours depending on the recipe.
